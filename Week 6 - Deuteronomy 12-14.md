# Deuteronomy - Week 6

## Worship on God's Terms: A Holy People

*Before the group meets, read Deuteronomy 12–14.*

# Main Theme

The God who chose Israel now tells them how He is to be worshiped, exclusively, on His terms, and with joy. Because they belong to Him as His treasured children, even their worship, their loyalty, and their daily lives must look different from the nations around them.

# Introduction

In chapters 9–11 Moses exposed Israel's lack of righteousness, pointed to God's grace, and called for a circumcised heart that loves the LORD. Beginning in chapter 12, that general call to love becomes specific. Moses lays out the detailed statutes Israel is to keep in the land, and he starts with worship.

Chapter 12 insists that Israel worship the true God in the way *He* chooses, not by copying the religion of Canaan and not by everyone "doing whatever is right in his own eyes." Chapter 13 then tests that loyalty where a prophet whose sign comes true must be rejected if he leads Israel toward other gods. Chapter 14 grounds all of it in identity. The people of Israel are "the sons of the LORD," a holy and treasured people, and that belonging is meant to shape even how they grieve and how they live. The rest of chapter 14 continues the same point: Israel's holiness shapes ordinary life, including what they eat and how they give.

As Israel stands at the border, Moses presses two truths:

- God alone sets the terms for how He is approached and worshiped.
- A people who belong to God are called to be visibly holy and distinct.



# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 12:1–4**

1. Why demand total destruction of the Canaanite worship sites, altars, pillars, and Asherim, rather than letting Israel simply ignore or reuse these places (vv. 2–3)?
2. It wasn't only the false gods that were forbidden, but worshiping the true God by Canaanite methods (v. 4). Why does God care not just about who Israel worships, but how?

**Read Deuteronomy 12:5–12**

3. What is the rule to worship only at the one place "the LORD will choose" (v. 5) meant to protect Israel from? How does it teach them that worship is received from God, not invented by people?
4. How does true worship hold strict boundaries and genuine joy together (vv. 7, 12)? What does that reveal about the God they're approaching?

**Read Deuteronomy 12:13–14**

5. Moses contrasts worship at "the place the LORD will choose" with everyone "doing whatever is right in his own eyes" (vv. 8, 13–14). What's the danger of each person deciding worship for himself, even with sincere intentions?

**Read Deuteronomy 13:1–4**

6. Moses says the LORD allowed this test "to know whether you love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul" (v. 3). What does this show about why God sometimes lets His people face tests of loyalty?
7. Verse 4 unpacks Israel's proper response with a string of actions: "walk after... fear... keep... obey... serve... hold fast." How does that verse define what it means to love God "with all your heart and with all your soul" (the Shema, 6:5)?

**Read Deuteronomy 14:1–2**

8. Israel is forbidden the pagan mourning customs of cutting themselves and shaving the forehead for the dead (v. 1), because they are "the sons of the LORD your God," "a people holy to the LORD," His "treasured possession" (vv. 1–2). Why would belonging to God affect something as personal as mourning? How does being God's holy children make them different from every other nation?



# Bringing the Passages Together

9. Worship at God's chosen place (ch. 12), loyalty under testing (ch. 13), and a holy way of life (ch. 14) all flow from one root: Israel belongs to the LORD. How does knowing whose they are shape how they worship and live? Why can't worship be left to personal preference?



# Application

10. Where are we tempted to approach God on our own terms rather than His? How do we tell the difference between worship God invites and worship we've simply invented?
11. Israel's identity as God's holy children was meant to be visible, even in how they grieved. As one of God's people, where is your life supposed to look noticeably different from the culture around you, and where have you quietly blended in instead?
