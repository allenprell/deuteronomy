# Deuteronomy - Week 4 (Leader's Guide)

## Chosen by Grace, Warned Against Forgetting (Deut 7-8)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- Have you ever been chosen for something, a team, a job, a friendship, and never quite understood why? How did being chosen for no obvious reason make you feel?
- When people ask you for help, what do they usually need help with?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 7:6-8**

**★ 1. Why does Moses go out of his way to ground Israel's identity as God's "treasured possession" in God's choice rather than in any quality of their own (v. 7)? What would change in how Israel sees itself?**

Moses pointedly says God did not choose them because they were impressive, "you were the fewest of all peoples" (v. 7). He cuts off pride at the root before they enter the land. If their status rests on God's choice and not their own strength, then they have nothing to boast about and nothing they could lose by failing to measure up. It shifts their self-image from achievers who earned a place to recipients who were simply loved. That humility is exactly what will keep them dependent once they're successful.

---

**★ 2. When Moses finally gives a reason for God's choice, the reason is simply that "the LORD loves you" and is "keeping the oath" He swore to their fathers (v. 8). What does this reveal about the nature of God's love, and how does it differ from the way human love and acceptance usually work?**

The reason for God's love turns out to be... His love. It's circular on purpose. He loves them because He loves them and because He made a promise. Human love is usually drawn out by something attractive, beauty, usefulness, shared interest, and it tends to last only as long as that quality does. God's love is not a response to anything in them; it originates entirely in Him. That makes it secure in a way human acceptance never is, because nothing in Israel created it and nothing in Israel can exhaust it.

---

**Read Deuteronomy 7:9-11**

**⚑ 3. How do God's steadfast love and His judgment fit together as one consistent character rather than as a contradiction (vv. 9-10)? Why does Moses say that knowing this God should make Israel "careful to do the commandment" (v. 11)?**

Verse 9 calls Him the faithful God who keeps steadfast love to a thousand generations; verse 10 says He repays those who hate Him. These aren't two moods; they're two expressions of one faithful character. A God who is utterly committed to His covenant must also oppose what destroys it. His love and His justice both flow from His faithfulness. Knowing such a God should make Israel careful, not terrified, because they're dealing with someone real and reliable whose commitment runs both directions. Taking Him lightly would be foolish given who He has shown Himself to be.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Would a love that never opposed anything actually be love, or just indifference?
- How does knowing God is both faithful and just make obedience a serious thing rather than an optional one?

**Read Deuteronomy 8:2-5**

**★ ⚑ 4. If God already knows their hearts, what is the purpose of a test that humbles them in the wilderness (v. 2)? What does the wilderness expose that comfort would have kept hidden?**

The test wasn't to inform God; it was to reveal Israel to themselves. Verse 2 says He led them to humble and test them "to know what was in your heart." Hardship strips away the pretenses and shows what we actually trust and how we actually respond. Comfort lets us imagine we're more faithful than we are; the wilderness pressed on them until what was really inside came out, grumbling, craving, but also dependence. God already knew; the testing was so they would know, and so the next generation could see plainly what their hearts were made of.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- The verse says the test was "to know what was in your heart." Whose knowledge is really at stake here?
- What do you learn about yourself in a hard season that a comfortable season would never show you?

---

**★ 5. What lesson about dependence was the daily manna meant to drill into them (v. 3)? When Jesus quotes this verse to resist temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1-4), what is He showing us about the lesson Israel was meant to learn?**

The manna came one day at a time so they couldn't stockpile or self-supply; every morning they were thrown back on God. The lesson: "man does not live by bread alone, but... by every word that comes from the mouth of the LORD" (v. 3). Real life comes from trusting and obeying God's word, not merely from securing physical needs. When Jesus quotes this in His own wilderness, hungry and tested, He shows He learned the lesson Israel kept failing: He would rather trust the Father's word than grasp for bread on His own terms. Where Israel grumbled, He trusted.

---

**6. How does framing forty years of hardship as a *father's* discipline rather than punishment change the way Israel should interpret their suffering (v. 5)? (See also Hebrews 12:5-11.)**

A father's discipline is an act of love aimed at his child's good, not the cold penalty of a judge. Verse 5 reframes the whole wilderness as God raising a son, not punishing a criminal. That changes everything about how they read their hardship: it was purposeful, corrective, and motivated by care, evidence of belonging rather than rejection. Hebrews 12 picks up the same logic, that God disciplines those He loves as sons. Suffering interpreted this way produces trust and maturity instead of bitterness.

**Read Deuteronomy 8:7-10**

**7. Why does Moses attach a command to *remember the Giver* to the description of the lavish gift of the good land (v. 10)? What danger is he already bracing them against?**

Right in the middle of describing how good the land will be, the brooks, the wheat, the bread without scarcity, Moses adds the command: "you shall eat and be full, and you shall bless the LORD your God" (v. 10). He attaches remembering to the gift because he knows the gift itself is what will tempt them to forget the Giver. The fuller they get, the easier it is to enjoy the blessing and lose sight of who gave it. He's bracing them against prosperity before it ever arrives.

**Read Deuteronomy 8:11-14, 17-18**

**★ 8. Why is prosperity, rather than hardship, the condition Moses treats as most spiritually dangerous (vv. 12-14)? How does being "full" make forgetting God so easy?**

Moses pictures the danger arriving precisely when life is good: full stomachs, fine houses, growing herds and silver (vv. 12-13). Then "your heart be lifted up, and you forget the LORD" (v. 14). Hardship makes us reach for God; abundance lets us feel self-contained. When every need is met, the sense of dependence fades, and a heart that doesn't feel its need quietly stops looking to God. The desert nearly killed them but kept them humble; the good land could prosper them and quietly ruin them.

---

**★ ⚑ 9. What is the subtle lie hidden in self-made success (v. 17), and why does Moses treat self-sufficiency as such a serious threat to covenant faith?**

The lie is the quiet thought, "My power and the might of my hand have gotten me this wealth" (v. 17). It's subtle because it doesn't deny God outright; it just rewrites the story so the credit lands on me. Moses counters that it is God "who gives you power to get wealth" (v. 18), even the ability to work was a gift. Self-sufficiency is dangerous because it's idolatry in disguise: it puts self in the place of God as provider, and a heart that credits itself feels no need to worship, thank, or obey. It's the seedbed of forgetting God altogether.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Notice the lie in verse 17 never mentions God. It just leaves Him out. Why is that more dangerous than openly denying Him?
- Verse 18 says even your ability to earn is a gift. How does that one truth dismantle the boast of verse 17?

**Read Deuteronomy 8:19-20**

**10. Why does Moses equate *forgetting* God with full-blown idolatry and destruction (vv. 19-20)? What does this tell us about how seriously the Bible treats a heart that simply drifts and loses its memory of God?**

Moses says if they forget God and go after other gods, they will "surely perish" like the nations being driven out (vv. 19-20). He treats forgetting and idolatry as the same trajectory because the heart never stays empty; if it loses its grip on the true God, it fills with false ones. Drifting isn't neutral. A heart that quietly stops remembering God is already on the road to serving something else. The Bible takes spiritual forgetfulness as seriously as outright rebellion because it ends in the same place.

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 11. Chapter 7 says Israel possesses everything by grace, and chapter 8 warns that they will be tempted to forget it the moment life gets comfortable. How do these two chapters work together? Why is *remembering that it was all a gift* the key defense against the pride that chapter 8 describes?**

This is the key question. Chapter 7 establishes the truth, they have nothing they didn't receive, and chapter 8 names the threat, prosperity will tempt them to forget it. The two fit like diagnosis and warning. Remembering that it was all a gift is the antidote to pride because pride only grows where we believe we earned something. You can't boast about what you were simply given. Keep the grace of chapter 7 in view and the lie of "my own hand did this" loses its grip. Forget the grace and pride rushes in.

*If you want to draw it out:*
- If you genuinely believed everything you have was a gift, what would be left to be proud of?
- Where does pride actually come from, having much or forgetting where it came from?

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**12. In your own areas of comfort, success, or security, where are you most tempted to quietly take the credit that belongs to God? What is one concrete way you could "bless the LORD" (8:10) and remember Him precisely there?**

The aim is for people to locate their own version of "my power and my hand" (8:17), the success they instinctively credit to themselves, and then turn it into an occasion for gratitude rather than pride. Push gently for one concrete practice of remembering, not a vague intention. Be ready to name your own area first; honesty here gives others permission to be honest too.

# Leader's Note

These two chapters answer two questions Israel was about to face: *why us?* and *what could ruin us?* The answer to the first is grace alone; the answer to the second is forgetting that grace. Keep both in front of your group. The wilderness almost broke them, but the good land could quietly destroy them, and the only safeguard is a memory that keeps crediting God. Help your group see that the comfortable seasons, not just the hard ones, are where faith is most tested.
