# Deuteronomy - Week 4

## Chosen by Grace, Warned Against Forgetting

*Before the group meets, read Deuteronomy 7–8.*

# Main Theme

God chose and redeemed His people purely out of love, not merit. Because everything they have is a gift, the great danger they face is not hardship but prosperity, the comfort that tempts them to grow proud and forget the God who gave it all.

# Introduction

In chapters 5–6 Moses gave Israel the commandments and called them to wholehearted love. Now he digs beneath that call to expose its foundation and its greatest threat.

Chapter 7 answers a question Israel might have asked: *Why us?* Moses insists their status as God's "treasured possession" rests on nothing in themselves, not their size, strength, or goodness, but only on God's love and His promise to their fathers.

Chapter 8 then looks forward to a surprising danger. Israel survived the wilderness; the harder test will be the good land. Once they are full, settled, and wealthy, they will be tempted to credit themselves and quietly forget the Lord. Moses presses the "remember and forget" theme that runs through the whole book to its sharpest point: forgetting God is not a small lapse, it is the road to ruin.

As Israel stands on the border, Moses sets two truths side by side:

- God's people are who they are entirely by grace.
- The blessings of grace can become the very thing that pulls the heart away from the Giver.

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 7:6–8**

1. Why does Moses go out of his way to ground Israel's identity as God's "treasured possession" in God's choice rather than in any quality of their own (v. 7)? What would change in how Israel sees itself?
2. When Moses finally gives a reason for God's choice, the reason is simply that "the LORD loves you" and is "keeping the oath" He swore to their fathers (v. 8). What does this reveal about the nature of God's love, and how does it differ from the way human love and acceptance usually work?

**Read Deuteronomy 7:9–11**

3. How do God's steadfast love and His judgment fit together as one consistent character rather than as a contradiction (vv. 9–10)? Why does Moses say that knowing this God should make Israel "careful to do the commandment" (v. 11)?

**Read Deuteronomy 8:2–5**

4. If God already knows their hearts, what is the purpose of a test that humbles them in the wilderness (v. 2)? What does the wilderness expose that comfort would have kept hidden?
5. What lesson about dependence was the daily manna meant to drill into them (v. 3)? When Jesus quotes this verse to resist temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1–4), what is He showing us about the lesson Israel was meant to learn?
6. How does framing forty years of hardship as a *father's* discipline rather than punishment change the way Israel should interpret their suffering (v. 5)? (See also Hebrews 12:5–11.)

**Read Deuteronomy 8:7–10**

7. Why does Moses attach a command to *remember the Giver* to the description of the lavish gift of the good land (v. 10)? What danger is he already bracing them against?

**Read Deuteronomy 8:11–14, 17–18**

8. Why is prosperity, rather than hardship, the condition Moses treats as most spiritually dangerous (vv. 12–14)? How does being "full" make forgetting God so easy?
9. What is the subtle lie hidden in self-made success (v. 17), and why does Moses treat self-sufficiency as such a serious threat to covenant faith?

**Read Deuteronomy 8:19–20**

10. Why does Moses equate *forgetting* God with full-blown idolatry and destruction (vv. 19–20)? What does this tell us about how seriously the Bible treats a heart that simply drifts and loses its memory of God?

# Bringing the Passages Together

11. Chapter 7 says Israel possesses everything by grace, and chapter 8 warns that they will be tempted to forget it the moment life gets comfortable. How do these two chapters work together? Why is *remembering that it was all a gift* the key defense against the pride that chapter 8 describes?

# Application

12. In your own areas of comfort, success, or security, where are you most tempted to quietly take the credit that belongs to God? What is one concrete way you could "bless the LORD" (8:10) and remember Him precisely there?
