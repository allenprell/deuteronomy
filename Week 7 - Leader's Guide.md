# Deuteronomy - Week 7 (Leader's Guide)

## Open Hands and Faithful Leaders: A People Under God's Word (Deut 15-18)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- When you've received unexpected generosity, did it make you more or less likely to be generous yourself? Why do you think that is?
- Think of a leader you've respected. Did their authority come from how much power they held, or from something else? What was it?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 15:7-11**

**1. How is Israel's generosity meant to mirror what God has already done for them in Egypt (v. 15)? What does remembering that they were once slaves God set free do to the way they hold their own possessions?**

Moses anchors the open hand in memory: "You shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the LORD your God redeemed you" (v. 15). Israel didn't earn their freedom or their land; both were given to people who had nothing. So generosity isn't charity from the secure to the needy, it's redeemed people passing along the grace they received. When you remember that everything you have came from a God who freed you, your grip loosens. You give the way you were given to.

---

**★ 2. Moses warns against the "wicked thought" of refusing to lend because the year of debt-release is near (v. 9), and commands giving "freely" with a heart that is "not grudging" (v. 10). What does this show about the kind of obedience God is after, and how someone could keep the letter of a law while breaking its heart? How does this connect to Moses' call back in chapter 10 to "circumcise" the heart?**

Every seventh year debts were canceled (vv. 1-2), and Moses anticipates the calculating heart that does the math: lending now means losing it soon, so the hand closes. He calls this a "wicked" thought and even names it sin (v. 9). Notice that the person who refuses to lend may be keeping the law's letter perfectly, no illegal interest, no broken rule, while violating its entire purpose. God wants the heart, not just the transaction: give "freely," not "grudgingly." This is exactly what circumcision of the heart (10:16) was about. Outward compliance with a cold heart isn't the obedience God is after; He wants the inner self reshaped so generosity flows willingly.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- The lender in verse 9 hasn't technically broken any rule. So why does Moses call his calculation sin?
- What's the difference between a gift given freely and the same gift given grudgingly? Does God see them the same way?
- How does this connect back to the "circumcised heart" of chapter 10, where outward obedience isn't enough?

---

**⚑ 3. Verse 4 says "there will be no poor among you," and then verse 11 says "there will never cease to be poor in the land." Since poverty will always be present, why does God still command an open hand instead of letting His people give up? And when Jesus quotes verse 11 (Mark 14:7), is He excusing neglect of the poor, or making a different point?**

The two statements aren't a contradiction; they're a condition and a reality. Verse 4 describes what would be true *if* Israel fully obeyed ("if only you will strictly obey," v. 5). Verse 11 names what will actually happen because they won't. So God doesn't say, "Since poverty is permanent, stop bothering." He says the opposite: "Therefore I command you, You shall open wide your hand" (v. 11). The ongoing need is precisely why ongoing generosity is commanded. When Jesus says "the poor you always have with you" (Mark 14:7), He's quoting this verse, not excusing neglect. He assumes His hearers know the rest of Deuteronomy 15:11 ("open wide your hand"). His point is that the unique moment of His anointing for burial was passing, while the opportunity to serve the poor would remain. He's defending the woman, not dismissing the poor.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Is verse 4 a promise or a condition? Look at verse 5, "if only you will strictly obey." How does that resolve the tension with verse 11?
- If poverty will never fully disappear, why would that be a reason to keep giving rather than a reason to quit?
- When Jesus quotes this verse, does He stop at "the poor you always have with you," or is He pointing His hearers to the whole command they already knew?

---

**Read Deuteronomy 17:14-20**

**★ 4. Israel will one day demand a king "like all the nations that are around me" (v. 14), the very thing Deuteronomy keeps warning them not to be. God permits a king, but bounds him with strict limits. How does God both allow Israel's request and guard against the worldly motive behind it? (Compare how this actually unfolds when Israel demands a king in 1 Samuel 8:4-9.)**

God doesn't forbid kingship outright; He regulates it in advance. He allows a king but insists he be one "whom the LORD your God will choose," an Israelite, not a foreigner (v. 15), and then surrounds the office with limits that directly target the "like the nations" impulse. The danger isn't the office, it's the motive. Israel wants a king to be like everyone else and to trust visible power. So God permits the institution while stripping out the very things that make pagan kings what they are. When the request finally comes in 1 Samuel 8, God tells Samuel they have rejected Him from being king over them, yet He still grants the request. He allows it, warns them what it will cost, and works through it anyway.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- What's actually wrong with wanting a king? Is it the king, or the reason they want one ("like all the nations")?
- In 1 Samuel 8 God says the people have rejected Him, yet He gives them a king anyway. How does that fit with the permission already written here in chapter 17?

---

**⚑ 5. The king is forbidden to multiply horses, wives, and silver and gold (vv. 16-17), a list King Solomon would later break almost point by point (1 Kings 10:14-11:8). If even the wisest king in Israel's history fell here, what does that tell us about how a divided heart actually takes hold, even in someone who knows better?**

The three prohibitions target the three classic engines of royal pride and compromise: military self-reliance (horses, especially trading back to Egypt for them, v. 16), political alliances and sensual excess (many wives, who "will turn away his heart," v. 17), and accumulated wealth (silver and gold, v. 17). Solomon, the wisest man alive, broke all three, and Scripture says plainly that "his wives turned away his heart" (1 Kings 11:4). The lesson is sobering: a divided heart rarely arrives in one dramatic act. It accumulates, one horse, one alliance, one compromise at a time, until the heart has quietly drifted. Knowing better is no protection when small allowances are never checked.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Verse 17 says the wives "will turn away his heart." Did Solomon's heart turn all at once, or by degrees? What does that pattern warn us about?
- Solomon had wisdom most of us will never have, and still fell here. What does that say about trusting our own strength to resist gradual compromise?

---

**★ 6. Unlike the god-kings of the nations, Israel's king must hand-write his own copy of God's law and read it "all the days of his life," so that "his heart may not be lifted up above his brothers" (vv. 18-20). What does it say about godly authority that Israel's most powerful man is placed under the same Word as everyone else? Why is pride named as the leader's chief danger, and how does daily submission to Scripture guard against it?**

The king's first royal duty isn't to conquer or build but to copy out the law in his own hand and then read it every day of his life (vv. 18-19). The most powerful man in Israel sits under the same Word as the lowest, which means in Israel no one is above the law because everyone is under God. The stated purpose is that "his heart may not be lifted up above his brothers" (v. 20). Pride is named as the leader's chief danger because authority constantly tempts a person to think the rules are for other people. Daily contact with God's Word is the antidote: it keeps reminding the king he is a brother under God, not a god over brothers.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- The king's job description starts with copying and reading Scripture, not ruling. What does that ordering reveal about where his authority is supposed to come from?
- Why is pride, rather than weakness or incompetence, singled out as the great danger for someone in power?

---

**Read Deuteronomy 18:15-22**

**7. Moses recalls that at Horeb the people were so terrified of hearing God's voice directly that they begged for a mediator (vv. 16-17), and God calls that request good, promising "a prophet like me." What does this reveal about how God deals with sinful people who cannot bear His unfiltered presence, while still making sure His Word reaches them?**

At Horeb the people pleaded not to hear God's voice or see the fire again, afraid they would die (v. 16). God doesn't rebuke them; He says, "They are right in what they have spoken" (v. 17), and provides a prophet to stand between them and speak His words. This shows a God who meets sinful people where they are. He knows they cannot survive His unmediated presence, so He graciously supplies a mediator, and yet He still makes sure His Word gets through. He neither overwhelms them nor leaves them in the dark. The prophet exists because God is both holy and determined to be heard.

---

**★ 8. God says of the coming prophet, "I will put my words in his mouth, and he shall speak to them all that I command him" (v. 18), and then warns that a prophet who speaks presumptuously must be tested and, if false, rejected (vv. 20-22). How do these verses define both the authority and the limits of anyone who claims to speak for God? How should this shape the way we weigh someone today who says, "God told me..."?**

A true prophet's authority is total but entirely borrowed: he speaks only the words God puts in his mouth, nothing added, nothing of his own (v. 18). That same standard sets the limit. A prophet who speaks presumptuously, claiming God's authority for his own words, or who speaks in the name of other gods, is to be rejected, and the test is whether the word comes true (vv. 20-22). So the claim to speak for God is taken with deadly seriousness *and* held to account. For today, this calls for discernment rather than either credulity or cynicism. When someone says "God told me," we don't accept it automatically because of the confidence behind it, and we don't dismiss it reflexively. We weigh it against what God has already said in His Word and against reality. Confidence is not credentials.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- The true prophet's words are completely God's, and that's also exactly what limits him. How can total authority and a strict limit be the same thing?
- What's the danger of accepting "God told me" simply because the person sounds sure? What's the danger of dismissing it out of hand?
- Where does verse 22 tell Israel to look to test a prophet's claim?

---

**9. The New Testament identifies the ultimate "prophet like Moses" as Jesus (Acts 3:22-23; John 1:45), and at the Transfiguration the Father commands, "This is my beloved Son... listen to him" (Luke 9:35). In what ways is Jesus like Moses, and in what ways is He far greater (see Hebrews 1:1-2)?**

Jesus is like Moses in real ways: He is a mediator between God and the people, He speaks the words God gives Him, and He delivers His people. But the likeness is also where the contrast begins. Moses spoke God's words; Jesus *is* the Word, the Son through whom God now speaks (Heb 1:1-2). Moses was a faithful servant in God's house; Jesus is the Son over the house. The prophets, including Moses, delivered messages; Jesus accomplishes the redemption they pointed toward. The Father's command at the Transfiguration, "listen to him," echoes Deuteronomy 18:15 and settles the matter: Jesus is the Prophet Israel was always meant to be waiting for.

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 10. These three passages reach into Israel's economic life (the poor), its political life (the king), and its spiritual life (the prophet). What single conviction ties them together about how God's people are meant to differ from the surrounding nations? And how do the law-keeping king and the word-speaking prophet both find their true fulfillment in Jesus?**

The single thread is that every part of Israel's life, money, leadership, and guidance, comes under God's character and His Word, and that is precisely what sets them apart from the nations. The nations let the powerful trample the poor; Israel opens its hand. The nations exalt kings as gods; Israel's king bows under the same law as everyone else. The nations consult diviners; Israel listens only to the prophet who speaks God's words. And the two great offices reach forward to Christ. He is the King who perfectly obeys the law no earthly king could keep, and He is the Prophet who not only carries God's word but is God's final Word to us.

*If you want to draw it out:*
- What do the poor, the king, and the prophet all have in common in these chapters? What is each one supposed to be brought under?
- Solomon broke the king's law and even true prophets only carried God's word secondhand. How does Jesus fulfill both offices in a way they never could?

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**11. Moses exposes the calculating heart that finds reasons not to help (v. 9) and calls instead for an open hand and an ungrudging spirit. Where are you most tempted to rationalize a closed hand toward someone in real need, and what specific excuse does your heart tend to reach for? What is one concrete step toward an open hand you could take this week?**

The aim is honesty about the "wicked thought" of verse 9, the quiet math we do to justify closing our hand. Help people name the actual excuse they reach for ("they'll waste it," "it's almost the end of the month," "someone else will handle it") rather than speak in generalities. Then push gently for one concrete step this week, not a vague intention. Be ready to share your own example to set the tone.

---

**★ 12. The king was to read God's Word daily so that his heart would not be "lifted up" and he would not "turn aside" (vv. 19-20). We are neither kings nor prophets, but the call to live under God's Word and truly listen to Christ is the same. This week, take one decision you're actually facing, put it under Scripture before you settle it, and let what God says shape the outcome. What is that decision, and what would it look like to listen?**

The point isn't to admire the king's discipline from a distance but to take up the same posture, settling decisions under God's Word rather than above it. Help each person name one real, current decision and then ask what genuinely "listening" would change about it. The aim is the king's humility ("his heart not lifted up") worked out in an ordinary choice this week. Keep it specific and personal, not hypothetical.

# Leader's Note

The thread through these chapters is simple to say and hard to live: every part of God's people belongs under His Word. Their wallets, their rulers, and the voices they trust all come under the same authority, and that's exactly what makes them unlike the nations. Keep steering the group back to that one conviction. And don't miss the forward look. The generous economy, the humble king, and the faithful prophet all strain toward Someone who fulfills them, the King who kept the law perfectly and the Prophet who is God's last and best Word. Help your group hear, in these old statutes, the command that still stands over us: "Listen to him."
