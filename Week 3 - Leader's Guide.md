# Deuteronomy - Week 3 (Leader's Guide)

## Covenant Love and Wholehearted Obedience (Deut 5-6)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- Think of a rule you grew up with that made no sense until you understood the reason behind it. What changed once you knew why?
- What's a habit you do almost without thinking, morning or evening, that quietly shapes your whole day?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 5:1-5**

**⚑ 1. What is Moses claiming about how the covenant relates to each new generation when he insists the covenant at Horeb was made "not with our fathers but with us, who are all of us here alive today" (v. 3)? Why does that matter as Israel stands at the edge of the land?**

Most of the people listening were children or unborn when Israel actually stood at Horeb, yet Moses says the covenant was made "with us." He is refusing to let them treat it as ancient family history that happened to someone else. The covenant lands on them personally; they are bound to God right now. As they prepare to enter the land, this matters because obedience cannot be inherited secondhand. Each generation has to own the relationship as its own.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Most of this crowd wasn't even at Horeb. Why does Moses still say the covenant was made "with us"?
- What's the difference between a faith you've inherited and one you've made your own?

**Read Deuteronomy 5:6-10**

**★ 2. Why does Moses ground the commandments in redemption already accomplished (v. 6) rather than in rewards Israel must earn? How should that reshape the way we hear the commands that follow?**

Before a single command is given, God identifies Himself: "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery" (v. 6). The commandments come to a people already rescued. Obedience is the response of the redeemed, not the price of redemption. That order is everything. It means the law is not a ladder Israel climbs to earn God's favor but the shape of life with a God who has already saved them. We hear the commands as a freed people, not as slaves trying to buy their way out.

---

**3. Recalling Moses' point in chapter 4 that Israel heard God's voice but saw no form, why does idolatry strike at the very foundation of Israel's covenant relationship with God, rather than being just one sin among many?**

The first commands deal with God Himself, no other gods, no images, before any command about people. Idolatry isn't one item on a list of misdeeds; it attacks the relationship at its root by replacing the true God with a false one. Since God revealed Himself by word and no form (chapter 4), making an image is both a lie about who He is and a transfer of loyalty. Get this wrong and everything downstream is corrupted, because you're no longer in covenant with the real God.

**Read Deuteronomy 5:11-21**

**⚑ 4. In Exodus 20 the Sabbath command is grounded in creation, but here Moses grounds it instead in Israel's slavery in Egypt and God's deliverance (v. 15). What does anchoring rest in redemption teach Israel about who God is, and what He intends their common life to look like?**

In Egypt they were slaves who never got a day off. Now their rest is to be a weekly memorial of their rescue: "you shall remember that you were a slave... and the LORD your God brought you out" (v. 15). It teaches that their God is a liberator, not a taskmaster. And it reshapes their society, because the command extends rest to servants, foreigners, and even animals (v. 14). A redeemed people are to build a community where no one is ground down the way they were in Egypt.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Who specifically gets to rest according to verse 14? What does that say about the kind of community God wants?
- If your weekly rest is a reminder you're no longer a slave, how does that change what rest is for?

---

**★ ⚑ 5. The final command forbids coveting (v. 21), an inward desire rather than an outward act. Why would the covenant reach past outward behavior into the heart's desires, and what does that reveal about the depth of what God actually requires of His people?**

The other commands can be broken by visible action, but coveting happens entirely inside, where no one else can see. By forbidding it, the law reveals that God is after the heart, not just clean conduct. Coveting is also the seed of the other sins; theft, adultery, and false witness all start as wrongful desire. This shows the covenant was never meant to be satisfied by outward compliance. God requires a heart that's genuinely His, which is exactly where chapter 6 will go next.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- You could keep the other nine and still break this one. What does that tell you about where real obedience lives?
- Notice how coveting feeds the commands just before it. Where do theft and adultery actually begin?

**Read Deuteronomy 6:4-5**

**★ 6. "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one" (v. 4) became the central confession of Israel's faith. Surrounded by nations who each worshiped many gods, what is Moses declaring about the LORD and about Israel's loyalty to Him?**

In a world of crowded pantheons where you could add a god for every need, Moses declares that the LORD stands alone. He is one, undivided and without rival, not one option among many. And because He is one, Israel's loyalty must be undivided too. You cannot give a whole heart to a God you treat as one deity among several. The oneness of God demands the wholeness of their devotion, which is exactly why the next verse calls for love with all the heart, soul, and might.

---

**★ 7. Jesus later called loving God with all your heart, soul, and might the greatest commandment (Mark 12:28-30). Why does Moses summarize covenant faithfulness as love rather than mere obedience, and why is wholehearted love the proper foundation for every command Israel has just received in chapter 5?**

This is one of the central questions of the study. Moses could have summed it up with "obey," but he says "love." That's because obedience without love is just compliance, and God wants more than a well-behaved people; He wants their hearts. Love is the right response to a God who first rescued them out of love. And love is the only foundation that holds the commandments together, because someone who truly loves God keeps the commandments from the inside out rather than grudgingly checking boxes. The commands of chapter 5 are meant to be the natural expression of the love commanded in chapter 6.

---

**Read Deuteronomy 6:6-9**

**★ 8. What does this picture of saturating the most ordinary moments of life with God's words reveal about where genuine covenant faithfulness is actually formed, and where it is most often lost?**

Moses doesn't locate faith in special religious events but in the ordinary rhythms: sitting at home, walking on the road, lying down, getting up (v. 7). Covenant faithfulness is formed in the daily, repeated, almost invisible moments, not mainly in the dramatic ones. Which means it's also most often lost there, not by a single big rebellion but by a slow neglect of God in everyday life. The home and the routine, not the sanctuary alone, are where the next generation either catches the faith or doesn't.

*If you want to draw it out:*
- Notice all four settings in verse 7 are completely ordinary. Why does Moses aim there instead of at religious ceremonies?
- If faith is mostly passed on in daily life, where is it most likely to quietly slip away?

**Read Deuteronomy 6:10-15**

**⚑ 9. Why is abundance often more spiritually dangerous than hardship? How does this connect to the theme of remembering and forgetting that runs through Deuteronomy?**

Moses warns that when they enter cities they didn't build and eat from vineyards they didn't plant (vv. 10-11), the danger is that they'll "forget the LORD" (v. 12). Hardship drives us to depend on God; abundance lets us feel we don't need Him. Comfort dulls the memory of who provided it. This is the recurring Deuteronomy pattern: the people forget God not in the desert but in the good land. The full stomach, not the empty one, is what most often loosens the grip on faith.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- When are you more likely to pray with urgency, when life is hard or when it's comfortable? Why?
- Everything in verses 10-11 is something they didn't earn. How does receiving good things you didn't work for tempt you to forget the Giver?

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 10. Chapter 5 gives the commandments, and chapter 6 gives the love from which obedience is meant to flow. How do these two passages fit together? Why would obedience without love, or claimed love without obedience, each fall short of what Moses is calling Israel to?**

The two chapters are two halves of one whole. Chapter 5 tells them what covenant life looks like; chapter 6 tells them where it comes from. Obedience without love is cold and eventually brittle, the behavior of a servant rather than a son. Claimed love without obedience is empty, because real love for God shows itself in keeping His words. Moses is calling for both held together: a love that obeys and an obedience that flows from love.

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**11. Practically, what would it look like for your love of God to move out of the abstract and into your ordinary routines this week?**

Personal, but tied directly to verses 6-9. The aim is concrete, not vague resolve. Push gently for the specific ordinary moments, the commute, the dinner table, the bedtime routine, where God's words could actually be present. Be ready to name your own example, including one you struggle with, to set an honest tone.

---

**12. Where in your own life of relative comfort are you most tempted to drift into spiritual forgetfulness, and what one specific practice from chapter 6 could help guard against it?**

This is where the warning of verses 10-15 lands. Help people name their own "good land," the comfort that quietly crowds God out, and then point them back to a tangible practice from the chapter: keeping God's words before them, talking of them at home, returning to the memory of His rescue. The goal is one honest area and one specific guard, not a long list.

# Leader's Note

Hold these two chapters together all evening: the commands of chapter 5 and the heart of chapter 6. Israel's great danger was never a lack of rules; it was a divided, forgetful heart. Keep steering the group back to the Shema. The God who is one rescued them out of love, and the only fitting response is to love Him with everything, in the ordinary places where life is actually lived. Help your group feel that the law was always an invitation into love, never a substitute for it.
