# Deuteronomy - Week 10 (Leader's Guide)

## Two Roads: The Blessing and the Curse of the Covenant (Deut 27-28)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- If you could turn any activity into an Olympic sport, what would you win a gold medal in?
- If you had to participate in an actual Olympic sport, which would you choose?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 27:1-8**

**1. Before Israel builds a single house in the land, Moses commands them to set up large stones, coat them with plaster, and write "all the words of this law" on them "very plainly", and he gives the command to write it down twice (vv. 3, 8). Given how often Deuteronomy warns that a settled, comfortable people will drift and forget God, why does He have them inscribe His words in plain sight the moment they arrive? How does that apply to us today?**

Comfort erodes memory. A people who get busy with houses, harvests, and full barns will slowly let God's words slip out of view, so God plants His word permanently and publicly before any of that begins. "Very plainly" means no one can claim it was hidden or hard to find. The standard is fixed, visible, and out where the whole nation has to walk past it. For us the principle holds: keep God's word central and in plain sight before the ease of life quietly crowds it to the margins.

**Read Deuteronomy 27:9-10**

**★ 2. Moses tells a people God rescued from Egypt forty years earlier, "this day you have become the people of the LORD your God" (v. 9), as if it were only now happening. What does he mean that they have *become* God's people on this particular day? And why does the next sentence put obedience second ("you shall *therefore* obey," v. 10) rather than making obedience the thing that turns them into God's people?**

This is the hinge of the whole ceremony, so slow down here. The covenant is being renewed and sealed with this generation, so "this day" makes their belonging present and binding; each generation has to enter it personally, not just inherit it. And notice the order. They don't obey in order to become God's people; they already are God's people, and *therefore* they obey. Belonging comes first as a gift, and obedience is the life that flows out of it. Get this order backward and the whole covenant turns into a system of earning. This is the same order the gospel runs on.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- What's the difference between obeying to become accepted and obeying because you already are?
- How does getting that order wrong turn faith into anxious performance?

**Read Deuteronomy 28:1-14**

**3. The blessings listed here, rain in season, crops, full barns (vv. 4-5, 11-12), are the very things Canaanite religion credited to Baal. Once Israel's survival in the land depends on the next harvest, what loyalty test does that set up? Where do you see that struggle play out later in their story?**

In Canaan, rain and fertility were exactly what the local gods promised to deliver. So the moment Israel's life depends on the next harvest, the temptation is to hedge their bets and add Baal to the mix, just in case. The test is whether they'll trust the LORD alone as the giver of rain and grain. It plays out across Israel's later history, most vividly on Mount Carmel, where Elijah forces the nation to choose between the LORD and Baal over the question of who actually sends fire and rain (1 Kings 18).

---

**★ 4. God promises more than full barns: He will make Israel "a people holy to himself," so that "all the peoples of the earth shall see that you are called by the name of the LORD" (vv. 9-10). How does this connect Israel's blessing back to God's promise that through Abraham's offspring all nations would be blessed? Why is Israel's prosperity meant to be *seen* by the surrounding nations rather than simply enjoyed?**

The blessing was never meant to stop with Israel's comfort. Back in Genesis, God promised Abraham that through his offspring all the families of the earth would be blessed (Gen 12:2-3). A flourishing, holy Israel was supposed to be a kind of display case, so that the watching nations would see what life under the true God looks like and be drawn to Him. Prosperity here has a purpose beyond enjoyment: it's a witness. Israel was to be God's advertisement to the world, not a private reward.

---

**Read Deuteronomy 28:58-68**

**5. Moses hangs this whole final section not on breaking specific rules but on whether Israel will "fear this glorious and awesome name, the LORD your God" (v. 58). Why does he locate the root of both obedience and disobedience in how Israel regards God Himself, rather than in the individual commands?**

Because the commands are symptoms, not the root. Reverence for who God is produces faithfulness across the board; lose that reverence, and the broken commands follow like fruit from a dying tree. Moses goes underneath the behavior to the heart's posture toward God. A person who genuinely fears the LORD will keep His words; a person who doesn't will eventually justify breaking any of them. So Moses names the source, not just the surface.

---

**⚑ 6. Verse 63 is difficult. Just as the LORD "took delight in doing you good," so He "will take delight in bringing ruin upon you." How can the same God delight in both?**

This isn't a picture of God enjoying cruelty. The point is that God stands fully behind His own justice with the same wholehearted commitment He brings to His goodness. He isn't reluctant or halfhearted about upholding His righteous character. The same faithfulness that pours out blessing on a faithful people will, with equal resolve, bring judgment on persistent, unrepentant betrayal. His "delight" is His settled commitment to do what is right, not a taste for suffering.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Would we really want a God who shrugged at evil and judged it only halfheartedly?
- How is God's resolve to judge wrong actually tied to His goodness rather than opposed to it?
- What keeps verse 63 from making God seem cruel? Notice it comes only after long, stubborn rebellion, not at the first stumble.

---

**7. The curses build to a final picture that reverses Israel's entire story: a people once "as numerous as the stars of heaven" left few (v. 62), scattered among the nations (v. 64), and at last carried "back in ships to Egypt" to be sold as slaves with no one even willing to buy them (v. 68). Why does Moses describe the worst possible ending as a return to Egypt?**

Egypt was the house of slavery God had rescued them from. To end up back there is the exodus run in reverse, the entire act of redemption undone. Everything God gave, freedom, land, multiplied descendants, is stripped away until they're standing where they started, only worse, because now no one will even buy them. Moses paints total covenant loss as a return to the place of bondage. It's the most devastating image he could choose, because it means the rescue itself has been forfeited.

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 8. Put the whole ceremony together: Israel is declared God's people and told to obey (27:9-10), commanded to keep His law ever before them in plain sight (27:1-8), with blessing and curse, life and ruin, all hanging on whether they do (ch. 28). Knowing how the story actually unfolds, that Israel forgets, breaks the covenant, and ends up under the very curses Moses lists, what does this whole ceremony expose about Israel, and why would something more than a covenant of "obey and live" eventually be needed?**

 The ceremony is righteous and clear: here is life, here is death, choose. But knowing how the story actually goes, that Israel forgets and walks straight into the curses, the ceremony quietly exposes the real problem. The issue isn't that the covenant is unfair; it's that the people cannot keep it. A covenant of "obey and live" can set the standard, but it can't change the heart that keeps failing. That's exactly the gap next week's chapters address with the promise of a circumcised heart (30:6), and ultimately it points to One who would bear the curse His people earned. Let the group feel the need before you resolve it.

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**9. The blessings climb to their peak in verses 28:11-14: overflowing prosperity, full barns, lending to many nations and borrowing from none. Yet that very abundance is what Deuteronomy treats as Israel's greatest spiritual danger, a comfortable, self-sufficient life that slowly forgets it ever needed God. Where is your own comfort or security most likely to make God feel optional? What would it look like this week to enjoy His gifts while staying genuinely dependent on the Giver?**

The aim is honesty about the strange danger of blessing. Most of us guard against hardship pulling us from God, but Deuteronomy keeps warning that prosperity is the bigger threat. Help people name the specific place, money, security, success, where having enough has quietly made God feel optional. Then steer toward the goal: not guilt over good gifts, but learning to enjoy them with open hands and a remembering heart. Be ready to name your own example first.

# Leader's Note

This is a heavy week, and it should be. The long list of curses can land hard, so don't soften it, but don't let the group stop there either. The whole ceremony sets two roads before Israel and dares them to choose, while quietly revealing they don't have it in them to walk the right one. Hold that tension. It's not meant to crush, but to create a longing for the heart God promises to give in the very next chapter. Let your group leave feeling both the weight of the covenant and the first hint of where the story has to go.
