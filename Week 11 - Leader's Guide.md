# Deuteronomy - Week 11 (Leader's Guide)

## Choose Life: The Heart God Must Give (Deut 29-30)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- Have you ever tried to change something about yourself by sheer willpower and found it just didn't stick? What was missing?
- When you've drifted away from something you really cared about, was it usually one big decision or a slow fade you barely noticed?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 29:2-9**

**⚑ 1. Why can't even a front-row seat to God's mighty acts produce a heart that truly knows Him? What is Moses saying Israel is missing when he says, "But to this day the LORD has not given you a heart to understand or eyes to see or ears to hear" (v. 4)?**

Knowing God isn't just a matter of information, and Israel proves it. They watched the plagues, walked through the sea, ate manna for forty years, and still Moses says they lack a heart to understand. The missing thing is internal, a God-given capacity to truly perceive and trust what they've seen. Evidence alone can't create that; it has to be given. Notice the phrase "the LORD has not given you," which quietly tells us where the solution will have to come from. This sets up the promise of 30:6.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- What's the difference between seeing something with your eyes and really grasping it in your heart?
- If forty years of miracles weren't enough, what does that tell us we actually need?

---

**2. Why does Moses keep rooting the command to obey in the memory of God's past faithfulness (vv. 5-9) rather than in the threat of the curses? What kind of obedience is he trying to produce?**

Moses reminds them that their clothes and sandals didn't wear out, that God sustained them and gave them victory (vv. 5-8), because he wants obedience born of gratitude and trust, not just fear of punishment. Fear can force compliance for a while, but it doesn't produce love, and it doesn't last. By pointing back to a faithful God who provided at every step, Moses aims to stir the kind of willing, affectionate obedience that flows from people who know they've been well cared for.

---

**Read Deuteronomy 30:1-10**

**3. Before Israel has even entered the land, Moses already foresees them breaking the covenant and being scattered (v. 1). Why would he build their failure into the covenant from the very start? What does that assume about Israel, and about anyone who stands before God's law?**

Moses isn't being pessimistic; he's being honest about the heart he just described in 29:4. He builds in their failure because he assumes it's coming, and so is the scattering. That assumption says something sobering about Israel, and about anyone who stands before God's commands: left to ourselves, we don't keep them. By weaving failure-and-restoration into the covenant from the outset, Moses signals that this arrangement always anticipated something beyond human willpower. Grace was built into the design.

---

**★ 4. Back in chapter 10 Moses commanded Israel to "circumcise the foreskin of your heart" (10:16), but here the wording changes: "the LORD your God will circumcise your heart" (30:6). What is different between the command and the promise, and who is doing the work now? Why is this the only real answer to the heart problem Moses named back in 29:4? (See also Jeremiah 31:33; Ezekiel 36:26-27.)**

This is the center of the whole study, so don't hurry past it. In chapter 10 the command put the work on Israel: *you* circumcise your heart. Here the promise hands the same work to God: *the LORD* will circumcise your heart. That shift is everything. The command exposes the need; the promise supplies what the command could never produce. And it's the only real answer to 29:4, because a heart that can't understand cannot operate on itself. God has to do the inner work, which is exactly what He later promises through Jeremiah and Ezekiel: a new heart and His Spirit within.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Why does a command, by itself, leave the deepest problem untouched?
- If the heart is the thing that's broken, what's wrong with just telling it to do better?

---

**5. How should the promise that God will "gather you again from all the peoples" (vv. 3-5) change the way Israel hears the curses of chapter 28?**

It means the curses, terrible as they are, are not the last word. Exile is real, but God promises to gather them back when they return to Him. That turns the curse from final annihilation into painful discipline with a way home on the far side. Israel can take the warnings of chapter 28 with full seriousness without being driven to despair, because the same God who warns of scattering promises to regather.

---

**Read Deuteronomy 30:11-20**

**⚑ 6. How can Moses call the word near and doable (vv. 11-14) when he has been so honest about Israel's hard heart (29:4)? How does the promise of 30:6 make sense of this? (Paul later applies these very words to the gospel in Romans 10:6-8.)**

The two only fit together because of the promise in between. In itself, the word isn't obscure or out of reach. It's not up in heaven or across the sea; it's right there in their mouth and heart, clear and knowable. But the doing of it becomes possible only once God circumcises the heart (30:6). So it isn't a call to grit it out by sheer effort; it's a call that rests on God-enabled response. That's why Paul can lift these very words and apply them to the gospel in Romans 10, "the word is near you," the word of faith that God Himself makes alive in us.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Is Moses saying obedience is easy, or that it's near and clear? What's the difference?
- How does verse 6 keep verses 11-14 from becoming "just try harder"?

---

**7. Why does Moses reduce all the laws of the previous chapters to two roads and a single decision: "life and good, death and evil" (v. 15)? What is he trying to make Israel think and feel as they stand on the border?**

After chapters of detailed statutes, Moses boils it all down to clarify what's actually at stake. Underneath every command is one fundamental allegiance: life with God or death apart from Him. He wants Israel standing at the border to feel the weight and urgency of the choice in front of them, not lost in the fine print. Two roads, one decision, and everything hangs on it.

---

**★ 8. Choosing life turns out to be choosing a Person, not just a set of rules (vv. 19-20). How does defining life this way change what obedience is really about? Why does Moses say God "is your life" rather than simply that God gives life?**

Moses defines choosing life as loving the LORD, obeying His voice, and holding fast to Him (v. 20). That reframes obedience entirely. It isn't rule-keeping for its own sake; it's clinging to the God who is the source of everything good. And he says God "is your life," not just that God gives life, because life was never a commodity to be handed over separate from Him. To have God is to have life; to turn from Him is death by definition. Obedience, then, is really about staying near the One you can't live without.

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 9. Put the three passages in order: Israel still has no heart to understand (29:4); God promises to circumcise their hearts so they can love Him (30:6); and on that basis Moses calls them to choose life (30:19). How does the promise in the middle hold together the inability at the start and the responsibility at the end? Why is this the same logic the gospel runs on, with God doing for us what we cannot so that we can then truly respond to Him?**

Hold the three pieces side by side. The inability is real (29:4), the responsibility is real (30:19), and on their own they seem to contradict, how can you command a people who can't obey? The promise in the middle resolves it. God does the very thing they cannot, changing the heart, and that's what makes a genuine response possible. This is exactly the shape of the gospel: God doesn't wait for us to fix ourselves before we respond, He gives the new heart, and then the call to choose Him is neither hopeless nor empty. Grace enables; then we truly answer.

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**10. Moses warns that the real danger is not only open rebellion but a heart that quietly "turns away" and is "drawn away" to other gods (vv. 17-18), the same slow drift Deuteronomy keeps returning to with its calls to remember and not forget. Where are you more prone to a quiet turning away from God than to any dramatic rejection of Him? What would "holding fast to him" (v. 20) look like in that specific area this week?**

Few of us renounce God outright; we drift. The aim is for people to spot the quiet erosion, the prayer life that thinned out, the affection that cooled, the substitute that crept in, rather than only watching for dramatic rebellion. Help them name one specific area and what "holding fast" would concretely look like there this week.

---

**11. Many of us treat obedience as gritting our teeth and trying harder. Moses grounds lasting obedience in a heart that God Himself changes (30:6), a promise fulfilled for us by the Spirit in the new covenant (Titus 3:5). If you really believed that the desire and the power to love God are gifts He gives rather than things you manufacture, what would change about the way you pursue obedience?**

Many people are quietly exhausted from white-knuckled religion. The aim is to move them from self-manufactured effort to dependence, asking God for the desire and power He promises to give, rather than trying to generate it themselves. The goal isn't passivity but a different starting point: prayer and trust before striving.

# Leader's Note

These two chapters are the hinge of the whole book. Everything Moses has urged finally runs into the wall of 29:4, that the people simply don't have the heart for it, and then breaks through into the promise of 30:6, that God will give what He commands. Keep that movement in front of your group the whole time: inability, promise, then the call to choose. If they leave understanding that the strength to love God is itself a gift from God, they've grasped not only Deuteronomy but the heart of the gospel it points toward.
