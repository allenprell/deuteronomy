# Deuteronomy - Week 7

## Open Hands and Faithful Leaders: A People Under God's Word

*Before the group meets, read Deuteronomy 15–18.*

# Main Theme

Every part of Israel's life in the land, their money, their leaders, and the voices they listen to, is to come under the rule of the LORD and the shaping of His Word. The same grace that redeemed them from slavery is meant to flow out through open hands to the poor, through a king who bows to God's law rather than ruling like the nations, and through prophets who speak nothing but God's own words. In the end these point past Israel to Jesus, the true King and the Prophet like Moses.

# Introduction

In chapters 12–14 Moses called Israel to worship God on His terms and to live as His holy, set-apart people. Chapters 15–18 carry that holiness out of the sanctuary and into the everyday structures of national life, the cancellation of debts and care for the poor (ch. 15), the yearly festivals (ch. 16), the appointment of judges and a king (ch. 16–17), and the provision for priests and prophets (ch. 18).

One conviction runs through all of it: Israel is not to be like the nations around them. The nations let the powerful trample the poor; Israel is to open its hand. The nations exalt their kings as gods; Israel's king must sit and read God's law like any other Israelite. The nations consult diviners and fortune-tellers; Israel is to listen only to the prophet through whom God Himself speaks.

This week's three passages each take up one slice of that life:

- **15:7–11** — an open-handed, generous heart toward the poor that reflects God's own generosity.
- **17:14–20** — a king unlike the kings of the nations, who fears the LORD and lives under His Word.
- **18:15–22** — a prophet like Moses, through whom God puts His own words into the mouth of His messenger.

As Israel stands at the border, Moses presses two truths:

- God means to govern every part of His people's life, their wallets, their rulers, and their guidance, by His own character and His Word.
- These offices and institutions point beyond themselves to Christ, the King who perfectly obeys and the Prophet we must hear.

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 15:7–11**

1. How is Israel's generosity meant to mirror what God has already done for them in Egypt (v. 15)? Why does grace received leave a person obligated to show grace?
2. Moses warns against the "wicked thought" of refusing to lend because the year of debt-release is near (v. 9), and commands giving "freely" with a heart that is "not grudging" (v. 10). What does this show about the kind of obedience God is after, and how someone could keep the letter of a law while breaking its heart? How does this connect to Moses' call back in chapter 10 to "circumcise" the heart?
3. Verse 4 promised "there will be no poor among you" if Israel fully obeyed, yet verse 11 admits "there will never cease to be poor in the land." The first describes the ideal; the second reckons with a world still marked by sin. Given that poverty will persist, why does God still command open-handed generosity rather than letting His people give up? When Jesus echoes verse 11, "you always have the poor with you" (Mark 14:7), is He excusing neglect, or saying something else?

**Read Deuteronomy 17:14–20**

4. Israel will one day demand a king "like all the nations that are around me" (v. 14), the very thing Deuteronomy keeps warning them not to be. God permits a king, yet hedges him in with strict limits. How does God both allow Israel's request and guard against the worldly motive behind it? (Compare how this actually unfolds when Israel demands a king in 1 Samuel 8:4–9.)
5. The king is forbidden to multiply horses, wives, and silver and gold (vv. 16–17), a list King Solomon would later break almost point by point (1 Kings 10:14–11:8). On the surface these look like a strong army, useful alliances, and wealth. What is dangerous about each of these for the man leading God's people, and what is each really tempting his heart to trust instead of the LORD?
6. Unlike the god-kings of the nations, Israel's king must hand-write his own copy of God's law and read it "all the days of his life," so that "his heart may not be lifted up above his brothers" (vv. 18–20). What does it say about godly authority that Israel's most powerful man is placed under the same Word as everyone else? Why is pride named as the leader's chief danger, and how does daily submission to Scripture guard against it?

**Read Deuteronomy 18:15–22**

7. Moses recalls that at Horeb the people were so terrified of hearing God's voice directly that they begged for a mediator (vv. 16–17), and God calls that request good, promising "a prophet like me." What does this reveal about how God deals with sinful people who cannot bear His unfiltered presence, while still making sure His Word reaches them?
8. God says of the coming prophet, "I will put my words in his mouth, and he shall speak to them all that I command him" (v. 18), and then warns that a prophet who speaks presumptuously must be tested and, if false, rejected (vv. 20–22). How do these verses define both the authority and the limits of anyone who claims to speak for God? How should this shape the way we weigh someone today who says, "God told me..."?
9. The New Testament identifies the ultimate "prophet like Moses" as Jesus (Acts 3:22–23; John 1:45), and at the Transfiguration the Father commands, "This is my beloved Son... listen to him" (Luke 9:35). In what ways is Jesus like Moses, and in what ways is He far greater (see Hebrews 1:1–2)? Why does it matter that God's final word to us comes through a Son rather than a servant?

# Bringing the Passages Together

10. These three passages reach into Israel's economic life (the poor), its political life (the king), and its spiritual life (the prophet). What single conviction ties them together about how God's people are meant to differ from the surrounding nations? And how do the law-keeping king and the word-speaking prophet both find their true fulfillment in Jesus?

# Application

11. Moses exposes the calculating heart that finds reasons not to help (v. 9) and calls instead for an open hand and an ungrudging spirit. Where are you most tempted to rationalize a closed hand toward someone in real need, and what specific excuse does your heart tend to reach for? What is one concrete step toward an open hand you could take this week?
12. The king was to read God's Word daily so that his heart would not be "lifted up" and he would not "turn aside" (vv. 19–20), and the people were commanded to listen to the Prophet God would send. We are neither kings nor prophets, but the call to live under God's Word and truly listen to Christ is the same. Honestly, is Scripture shaping your decisions and humbling your heart day by day, or have you drifted into hearing it without really listening?
