# Deuteronomy - Week 2 (Leader's Guide)

## The LORD Alone Is God (Deut 4)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- If you had to teach one subject. What are you teaching?
- You suddenly are gifted with any skill of your choice. What is that skill?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 4:1-14**

**⚑ 1. How does Moses frame obedience in verses 1 and 4, as burden, duty, privilege, or wisdom? What in the text shapes your answer?**

Moses frames obedience as the path to life. In verse 1 he urges them to keep the statutes "that you may live, and go in and take possession of the land," and in verse 4 he points to those "who held fast to the LORD your God" as the ones "all alive today." Obedience here is not a heavy load laid on them; it is how they stay tethered to the God who gives life. The categories aren't mutually exclusive, but the text leans hard toward life and privilege rather than mere duty.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- What's the result of obedience in verse 1? What's the result of clinging to God in verse 4?
- Is Moses describing obedience as the price of life in the land, or as the shape of a life already given by grace?

---

**★ 2. Moses says the surrounding nations will see Israel's laws and conclude that Israel's God is near and wise (vv. 5-8). What does this reveal about God's purpose for Israel among the nations?**

Israel was never meant to be an end in itself. Their obedience was to be a public display, a kind of exhibit, that would make the watching nations ask what kind of God could be so near to His people and give laws so righteous (vv. 7-8). God's purpose was that through this one nation's life the world would catch a true picture of who He is. This echoes the promise to Abraham that all nations would be blessed through his offspring. Israel's wisdom was not for hoarding; it was a witness.

---

**3. What specifically does Moses tell the people to pass on to their children and grandchildren (vv. 9-10), and why does he frame forgetting as such a serious danger?**

They are to pass on what their own eyes saw, above all the day they stood before God at Horeb and heard His voice out of the fire (vv. 9-10). The danger of forgetting is that faith is only ever one generation from being lost. If the children never receive the memory, they will have no firsthand knowledge of God to anchor their loyalty, and the slide toward idolatry becomes almost inevitable. Forgetting is not a harmless lapse; it is how a covenant people quietly come undone.

**Read Deuteronomy 4:15-24**

**⚑ 4. Why is the detail that Israel heard God's voice but saw no form so important to Moses' warning against idolatry (vv. 15-19)?**

Because it removes any excuse for making an image. At Horeb God revealed Himself by word, not by visible shape; they "saw no form." So any image they might carve would not represent the God who actually appeared. It would be an invention. Moses grounds the whole prohibition in what they did and did not experience that day: they heard a voice, so they are to listen and obey, not to look and replicate.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- If God deliberately showed no form, what is anyone claiming about Him the moment they make one?
- How does "you saw no form" turn the second commandment from an arbitrary rule into something that fits who God revealed Himself to be?

---

**★ ⚑ 5. Israel was forbidden from representing God through any image, whether of humans, animals, or celestial bodies (vv. 16-19). What does this prohibition imply about the kind of being God is? Why would any image inevitably misrepresent Him?**

This is one of the central questions of the study. The sweep of the list, every creature on land, in the sky, in the water, and even the sun, moon, and stars, makes the point that nothing in creation can stand in for the Creator. God is not part of the created order; He made all of it. Any image necessarily drags the infinite, living God down to the level of a thing He made, which both shrinks Him and lies about Him. An idol is always smaller, more manageable, and more controllable than the real God, and that is precisely its appeal and its danger.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- What does it do to our sense of God when we picture Him as something we can see, hold, or carry?
- Notice the list moves all the way up to sun, moon, and stars. Is there anything in creation big enough to picture God without misrepresenting Him?
- An image you make is one you can manage. How might that be the real attraction of idolatry?

---

**6. What does describing God as "a consuming fire" and "a jealous God" (v. 24) communicate about God's relationship to Israel, and how do they explain the weight of the warnings Moses has just given?**

Both pictures describe a God who will not share His people's worship. "Jealous" is not pettiness; it is the rightful response of a husband to a wife's unfaithfulness, the language of a real covenant relationship. "Consuming fire" warns that His holiness is not safe to trifle with. Together they explain why idolatry is treated so severely: it is covenant betrayal of the most personal kind, against a God who is both deeply committed and utterly holy.

**Read Deuteronomy 4:25-31**

**★ 7. Moses prophesies that Israel will fall into idolatry and be scattered among the nations, yet promises that when they seek God in exile, they will find Him (vv. 29-31). What does this passage reveal about the nature of God's covenant commitment to Israel?**

Moses sees the failure coming and yet does not see the relationship ending. Even after Israel breaks faith and is scattered, if they seek God with all their heart they will find Him, because "the LORD your God is a merciful God. He will not leave you or destroy you or forget the covenant" (v. 31). God's commitment rests on His own faithfulness and His oath to the fathers, not on Israel's performance. Judgment is real, but it is not the last word. This is a striking early glimpse of grace that survives even exile.

**Read Deuteronomy 4:32-40**

**⚑ 8. What is Moses arguing by inviting Israel to survey all of history and ask whether anything like what God did for them has ever occurred (vv. 32-34)? What does he want Israel to conclude?**

He is building a case from the uniqueness of their experience. Has any people ever heard God speak from the fire and lived? Has any god ever reached into the middle of another nation and pulled out a people for himself by signs, wonders, and a mighty hand? The expected answer is no, never. Moses wants Israel to reason from the evidence: a God who did the unprecedented for them is unlike every so-called god of the nations, and therefore deserves their exclusive loyalty.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Moses isn't asking them to feel something; he's asking them to weigh evidence. What's the verdict he expects?
- If no other nation has a story like this, what should that settle about which God is worth worshiping?

---

**★ 9. According to verses 35 and 39, what conclusions does Moses want Israel to reach about God? How do those conclusions reframe the warnings and commands Moses has given throughout the chapter?**

Both verses press the same conclusion: "the LORD is God; there is no other besides him" (v. 35), and they are to "lay it to your heart" that He is God in heaven and on earth, "there is no other" (v. 39). This is the foundation the whole chapter has been building toward. Once you know there simply is no other God, every command against idolatry stops being arbitrary. You don't worship images because there is no rival to the LORD, and you obey Him because He alone is real and alive.

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 10. Looking across the whole chapter, what case does Moses make for why Israel should obey the LORD and worship Him alone? What are the main pillars of his argument?**

Obedience leads to life and is a witness to the nations (vv. 1-8). They saw no form, so He cannot be imaged (vv. 15-19). He is a jealous, consuming-fire God who is also merciful and faithful to His covenant (vv. 24, 31). And above all, history itself proves there is no God but the LORD (vv. 32-39). The argument is not "obey because I said so." It is "worship Him alone because of who He has shown Himself to be."

*If you want to draw it out:*
- Which of these pillars would land hardest on a people about to enter a land full of idols?
- Notice how Moses argues from what God has done before he commands. Why is that order important?

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**11. What do Moses' prescriptions for guarding against forgetting, teaching their children, recalling what they witnessed at Horeb, and keeping God's statutes, reveal about what forgetting actually looks like in practice? How do they translate to the life of a believer today?**

Personal, but rooted in the text. Forgetting God is rarely a sudden denial; it's a slow neglect of the practices that keep Him in view. The aim is for people to see that remembering takes deliberate effort: telling the next generation, returning often to what God has done, staying in His word. Help them name the practices that actually keep their own memory alive, and the neglected ones that let it fade.

---

**12. What is one truth about God from Deuteronomy 4 that you most want to remember this week?**

Personal. The goal is for each person to walk away with one concrete truth, not a vague impression. It might be His nearness (v. 7), His mercy that outlasts failure (v. 31), or that He alone is God (v. 39). Be ready to share your own first to set the tone.

# Leader's Note

Chapter 4 sits at a hinge in the book. Before Moses reviews the law, he fixes their eyes on the Lawgiver. Keep pulling the group back to the one conclusion the whole chapter exists to produce: the LORD is God, and there is no other. Every warning against idols and every call to obey flows from that single truth. If your group leaves knowing God a little more clearly, the rest of Deuteronomy will land where it should.
