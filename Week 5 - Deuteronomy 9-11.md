# Deuteronomy - Week 5

## Not by Your Righteousness: Grace That Changes the Heart

*Before the group meets, read Deuteronomy 9–11.*

# Main Theme

Israel receives everything by grace and nothing by merit, in fact they are a stubborn people. Yet this grace is not meant to leave the heart unchanged. The God who chose them in love calls them to a circumcised heart that fears Him, loves Him, and shows that love in how they live and how they treat the vulnerable.

# Introduction

In chapters 7–8 Moses warned Israel against the pride that creeps in with prosperity, the lie that says, "My power and my hand earned this." Now Moses presses even deeper. It is not only that Israel must not take credit for their wealth; they cannot take credit for the land at all. They are not being brought in because they are good. They are, in Moses' own words, "a stubborn people."

Chapter 9 dismantles any notion of Israel's righteousness, using the golden calf and a string of rebellions as evidence. Chapter 10 then turns from what Israel is *not* to what God *is*: the God who owns everything yet set His heart in love on this one small nation, the God of overwhelming greatness who also defends the orphan, the widow, and the foreigner. Out of that grace flows the great summary of the covenant, "what does the LORD your God require of you?", and the startling command to "circumcise the foreskin of your heart." Chapter 11 then calls Israel, once more, to lay up these words in their hearts and pass them to their children.

As Israel stands at the border, Moses holds two truths together:

- Israel possesses nothing by righteousness and everything by grace.
- Grace is meant to produce a transformed heart that loves God and loves others.

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 9:4–6**

1. Three times in three verses Moses says Israel is not getting the land "because of your righteousness" (vv. 4–6). Why drive the point home so hard right when they're about to succeed? What wrong conclusion is he trying to head off?

2. Moses gives two real reasons they're entering the land: the nations' wickedness and God's oath to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (v. 5). Then he calls Israel "a stubborn people" (v. 6). How does that label change the way they should see the gift?

**Read Deuteronomy 10:12–22**

3. Moses asks, "What does the LORD your God require of you?" and answers with fear, love, and wholehearted service (vv. 12–13), yet calls these commands "for your good." How can demands like these be for Israel's good rather than for God's benefit? How should that reshape the way we hear His commands?

4. Everything in heaven and earth already belongs to God (v. 14), yet He "set his heart in love" on Israel and chose them (v. 15). Given that He could choose anyone, what does choosing one small, stubborn people say about the kind of love He has?

5. Moses commands Israel to "circumcise... the foreskin of your heart" (v. 16), taking the outward sign of belonging to God's people and applying it to the heart. What is he actually calling for, and why isn't an outward mark of belonging enough? (See Deuteronomy 30:6; Jeremiah 4:4; Romans 2:28–29.)

6. Why tie God's greatness as the "God of gods and Lord of lords" (v. 17) to His care for the orphan, the widow, and the sojourner (v. 18)? What would we get wrong if we held only one of these truths?

7. How does remembering their own rescue in Egypt shape the way they treat outsiders (v. 19)? What pattern does that set for God's redeemed people?

8. Israel's fathers went down to Egypt as just "seventy persons", but now they are "as numerous as the stars of heaven" (v. 22). Why does Moses point back to God's promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:5) here? How does seeing themselves as the fulfillment of that promise reinforce his point about grace?

**Read Deuteronomy 11:18–25**

9. The command to keep God's words in the heart, teach them to the children, and write them on the doorposts (vv. 18–20) repeats chapter 6 almost word for word. Why say it again? What does that repetition tell us about how faith is preserved or lost from one generation to the next?

10. Moses links keeping the land to "loving the LORD your God... and holding fast to him" (vv. 22–23), yet earlier he said the land was *not* earned by righteousness (9:4–6). How do both fit together: the land as pure gift, yet tied to obedience?

# Bringing the Passages Together

11. This week moves from "you have no righteousness of your own" (ch. 9) to "circumcise your heart" and "love the LORD" (ch. 10–11). How does grace actually *produce* obedience rather than make it unnecessary? Why is that the opposite of trying to earn God's favor?

# Application

12. Moses calls for love that reaches the heart (10:16) and shows up in how we treat the vulnerable (10:18–19). Where is God calling you past going-through-the-motions religion into a genuinely changed heart? And who is the "sojourner", an outsider or overlooked person, in front of you to love because you've been shown grace?
