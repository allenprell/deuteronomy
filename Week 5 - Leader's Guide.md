# Deuteronomy - Week 5 (Leader's Guide)

## Not by Your Righteousness: Grace That Changes the Heart (Deut 9-11)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- What book besides the Bible has most impacted you?
- What's the first thing you'd buy if you won the lottery today?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 9:4-6**

**★ 1. Three times in three verses Moses says Israel is not getting the land "because of your righteousness" (vv. 4-6). Why drive the point home so hard right when they're about to succeed? What wrong conclusion is he trying to head off?**

Success is exactly the moment self-congratulation creeps in. As the victories pile up, Israel will be tempted to read them as proof of their own goodness, "I must have deserved this." Moses repeats himself three times to slam that door shut before it opens. The wrong conclusion he's heading off is self-righteousness, the idea that they earned the land by being better than the nations they're replacing. He wants the win itself to leave them humble rather than proud.

---

**2. Moses gives two reasons they're entering the land: the nations' wickedness and God's oath to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob (v. 5). Then he calls Israel "a stubborn people" (v. 6). How does that label change the way they should see the gift?**

Both reasons point away from Israel: the land is being taken from the nations because of *their* wickedness, and given to Israel because of God's *promise to the fathers*, not Israel's virtue. Then Moses removes any remaining illusion by calling them "a stubborn people" (v. 6). The label forces an honest self-assessment. They aren't the deserving heroes of the story; they're recipients of a gift they had no right to. That should produce gratitude and humility rather than entitlement.

**Read Deuteronomy 10:12-22**

**★ ⚑ 3. Moses asks, "What does the LORD your God require of you?" and answers with fear, love, and wholehearted service (vv. 12-13), but calls these commands "for your good." How can demands like these be for Israel's good rather than for God's benefit? How should that reshape the way we hear His commands?**

God doesn't need anything from Israel; He owns heaven and earth (v. 14). So when He commands fear, love, walking in His ways, and wholehearted service, these are not tributes He requires for Himself but the very things that make human life flourish. Verse 13 says plainly they are "for your good." We were made to know and love God, so living that way is life working as designed. This reshapes how we hear every command: not as a tax God collects but as a good Father showing His children how to truly live.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Verse 14 says God already owns everything. So what could He possibly gain from your obedience?
- If a command is genuinely for your good, does that change whether you'd call it a burden?

---

**★ 4. Everything in heaven and earth already belongs to God (v. 14), yet He "set his heart in love" on Israel and chose them (v. 15). Given that He could choose anyone, what does choosing one small, stubborn people say about the kind of love He has?**

The God who owns all peoples wasn't looking to gain anything when He set His heart on Israel. He had His pick of the whole earth and chose a small, stubborn nation. That tells us His love is free and unforced, drawn out by nothing in them and aimed entirely at their good. It's the love of a giver, not a buyer. He didn't choose the impressive; He chose the undeserving, which means the choosing says everything about His heart and nothing about their worth.

---

**★ ⚑ 5. Moses commands Israel to "circumcise... the foreskin of your heart" (v. 16), taking the outward sign of belonging to God's people and applying it to the heart. What is he actually calling for, and why isn't an outward mark of belonging enough? (See Deuteronomy 30:6; Jeremiah 4:4; Romans 2:28-29.)**

Circumcision was the physical mark that you belonged to God's covenant people. By moving it to the heart, Moses is saying that the outward sign was never the point; God wants an inward reality. He's calling for a heart stripped of its stubbornness and genuinely devoted to God, the very thing verse 16 names: "be no longer stubborn." An external mark can sit on a rebellious heart, which is exactly Israel's problem. The later passages confirm this: real belonging is a matter of the heart, and Deuteronomy 30:6 even hints that God Himself will one day do this circumcising.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- You could carry the outward sign and still have a hard, stubborn heart. So what is Moses really after?
- Notice verse 16 pairs "circumcise your heart" with "be no longer stubborn." What does that tell you it means?
- Deuteronomy 30:6 says God will circumcise their hearts. What does it suggest that the thing commanded here is something God ultimately has to do?

---

**★ 6. Why tie God's greatness as the "God of gods and Lord of lords" (v. 17) to His care for the orphan, the widow, and the sojourner (v. 18)? What would we get wrong if we held only one of these truths?**

Moses deliberately joins God's overwhelming greatness to His tenderness toward the powerless. The same God who is mighty and awesome and takes no bribe is the one who feeds the orphan and loves the sojourner. If we held only His greatness, we'd picture a distant, intimidating power with no heart for the small. If we held only His compassion, we'd shrink Him into something soft and harmless. The text insists on both: He is great *and* He bends down to the vulnerable, and His greatness is precisely what makes His care so striking.

---

**7. How does remembering their own rescue in Egypt shape the way they treat outsiders (v. 19)? What pattern does that set for God's redeemed people?**

Moses grounds the command to love the sojourner in memory: "for you were sojourners in the land of Egypt" (v. 19). Having been the vulnerable outsiders themselves, and having been shown mercy, they're now to extend that same mercy to outsiders among them. The pattern is that grace received becomes grace given. God's people are meant to treat others the way God treated them. A community that forgets it was once helpless tends to grow hard toward the helpless.

---

**8. Israel's fathers went down to Egypt as just "seventy persons", but now they are "as numerous as the stars of heaven" (v. 22). Why does Moses point back to God's promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:5) here? How does seeing themselves as the fulfillment of that promise reinforce his point about grace?**

God had promised Abraham descendants as countless as the stars, and the crowd standing in front of Moses is that promise come true. Seventy became a multitude. By pointing this out, Moses shows that their very existence as a nation is a gift God produced, not an achievement they earned. They are living proof that God keeps His word across centuries. That reinforces the whole theme: everything they are traces back to grace and promise, not to their own doing.

**Read Deuteronomy 11:18-25**

**9. The command to keep God's words in the heart, teach them to the children, and write them on the doorposts (vv. 18-20) repeats chapter 6 almost word for word. Why say it again? What does that repetition tell us about how faith is preserved or lost from one generation to the next?**

Moses repeats it because it's that important and that easily neglected. Faith isn't preserved automatically; it has to be deliberately laid up in the heart and actively handed to the children, woven into daily life and the home. The repetition signals that this is the hinge on which each generation turns. When a generation stops embedding God's words in ordinary life and stops teaching the next one, faith doesn't survive by inertia; it quietly dies. Saying it twice underlines that there's no coasting in this.

---

**★ ⚑ 10. Moses links keeping the land to "loving the LORD your God... and holding fast to him" (vv. 22-23), yet earlier he said the land was *not* earned by righteousness (9:4-6). How do both fit together: the land as pure gift, yet tied to obedience?**

This feels like a contradiction but isn't. The land is given purely by grace; they never earned the right to receive it (chapter 9). But once given, life in the land is meant to be lived in covenant faithfulness, loving God and holding fast to Him. Obedience isn't the price that purchases the gift; it's the way a grateful people stay in the place of blessing. Think of it as the difference between earning a home and living rightly in a home you were freely given. Grace grants it; love keeps faith with the One who gave it.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- Is obedience here the way they *earn* the land, or the way they *keep faith* with the God who gave it?
- A gift you're given still has to be lived in. How is staying faithful in the land different from earning it in the first place?

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 11. This week moves from "you have no righteousness of your own" (ch. 9) to "circumcise your heart" and "love the LORD" (ch. 10-11). How does grace actually *produce* obedience rather than make it unnecessary? Why is that the opposite of trying to earn God's favor?**

The flow of these chapters is the whole point: because Israel has nothing to boast in (ch. 9), their only fitting response to being loved is wholehearted love in return (ch. 10-11). Grace doesn't make obedience pointless; it gives it the right motive. Earning works from fear and self-interest, trying to get God to owe you something. Grace-driven obedience works from gratitude, responding to a love already given. One tries to buy God's favor; the other lives out of favor already received. Help the group feel that difference, because it's the difference between religion and relationship.

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**12. Who is the "sojourner", an outsider or overlooked person, in front of you to love because you've been shown grace?**

Push gently toward one real person who could be loved as the "sojourner." The motive throughout is grace received becoming grace given. Be ready to go first and name your own.

# Leader's Note

These chapters take the grace theme deeper than ever. It isn't only that Israel mustn't credit their wealth to themselves (chapters 7-8); they can't claim the land at all, because they're a stubborn people who possess everything by grace. But grace was never meant to leave the heart untouched. Keep both halves together all evening: there is no righteousness of our own, and yet God means to circumcise the heart so that we genuinely love Him and love the overlooked. Help your group see that grace and a changed life are not rivals. The first produces the second.
