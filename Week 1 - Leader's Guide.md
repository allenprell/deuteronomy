# Deuteronomy - Week 1 (Leader's Guide)

## Remember God's Faithfulness (Deut 1-3)

*This guide is for the leader's preparation. It gives a sense of where each question is heading, flags the questions worth lingering over, and offers extra prompts where the discussion may stall. Use the answers as a compass, not a script. The goal is for the group to discover these things in the text, not to hear you recite them.*

# How to Use This Guide

- **★ marks the questions worth spending the most time on.** They carry the weight of the study. If you run short on time, protect these.
- **⚑ marks questions that tend to be difficult.** Extra follow-up prompts are provided to help you keep the conversation moving.
- Brief answers are given for every question so you can confirm the group is landing in the right place.

# Icebreaker Options

Pick one to open. Keep it short, the real work is in the text.

- Think of a time someone reminded you of something you already knew but had forgotten in the moment. How did that reminder change what you did next?
- When you're facing something hard, are you more likely to focus on the size of the problem or on the resources you have to meet it? Why do you think that is?

# Understanding the Text & the Narrative

**Read Deuteronomy 1:19-33**

**1. Why does Moses begin his sermon by revisiting the failure at Kadesh-barnea nearly forty years ago instead of focusing on Israel's victories or successes?**

The generation in front of Moses needs to understand why the previous generation died in the wilderness. Their parents refused to trust God at the very threshold of the land, and that unbelief cost them everything. Moses is not shaming them; he's warning them. They stand at the same threshold now. Honest memory of failure is meant to keep them from repeating it.

---

**★ ⚑ 2. In verses 26-28, the people's fears are centered on the strength of the enemy and the difficulty of the task. What truths about God in verses 29-33 should have changed the way they evaluated the situation? Did the Israelites know these truths?**

This is one of the central questions of the study. Moses points to three things: God goes before them and fights for them (v. 30), God already acted for them in Egypt (v. 30), and God carried them through the wilderness "as a man carries his son" (v. 31). The point is that they had *firsthand evidence* of God's power and care. They were not asked to trust a stranger. They knew these truths, which makes their fear a failure of faith rather than a lack of information.

*If the group is quiet, try:*
- They had seen the plagues, the Red Sea, and daily provision. Why didn't that evidence settle their fears?
- What's the difference between knowing something is true and trusting it when you're afraid?
- Notice v. 32: "you did not believe the LORD your God." Where does Moses locate the real problem, in the size of the enemy or somewhere else?

---

**★ 3. Verses 30-33 emphasize God's past actions, present presence, and future promises. What does this teach us about the relationship between remembering God's faithfulness and trusting Him in the present?**

Remembering is not nostalgia. What God has already done is the ground for trusting what He has promised to do. The past gives evidence, the present gives His company, and that combination is meant to fuel confidence about the future. Faith looks backward in order to step forward. This sets up the theme that runs through the whole book.

**Read Deuteronomy 2:7**

**4. Why would Moses' reminder that "the LORD your God has been with you" and that they "have lacked nothing" be particularly important for a people preparing to enter a season of warfare and conquest?**

War exposes need: food, protection, endurance, morale. Moses reminds them that across forty years of wilderness, God supplied every need and they lacked nothing. The God who sustained them in the desert will not abandon them in battle. The reminder is meant to steady them before the hardest test yet.

**Read Deuteronomy 3:21-29**

**5. Moses points Joshua back to what God has already done before speaking about what God will do next. What principle of faith and leadership is Moses modeling for Israel and for Joshua?**

Moses tells Joshua to remember what God did to the two kings, Sihon and Og, because God will do the same to all the kingdoms ahead (v. 21-22). Past victories become the basis for future courage. Good leadership doesn't manufacture confidence; it directs people's eyes to what God has already proven about Himself. Joshua is being trained to lead the way Moses has led, by memory and trust rather than fear.

---

**⚑ 6. Why was Moses forbidden from entering the promised land? Read Numbers 20:2-13. What were Moses' sins?**

At Meribah the people grumbled for water. God told Moses to *speak* to the rock; instead Moses struck it twice and said, "shall *we* bring water for you out of this rock?" (Num 20:10-11). His sins were disobedience to God's specific command, a flash of anger toward the people, and a failure to honor God as holy before Israel, taking to himself credit that belonged to God. God says Moses and Aaron "did not believe" Him (Num 20:12), tying Moses' failure to the same root sin that doomed the wilderness generation: unbelief.

*This one can feel harsh to the group. If someone pushes back that the punishment seems severe, try:*
- Moses was Israel's leader and mediator. Why might God hold a leader to a stricter account in front of the whole nation?
- Notice that the charge against Moses ("did not believe me") is the same charge against the people in chapter 1. What does that connection suggest about the seriousness of unbelief, even in a faithful man?
- Does Moses' exclusion make God seem unjust, or does it show that no one, not even Moses, stands above God's holiness?

# Bringing the Passages Together

**★ 7. Why should "remembering" be such a central theme in the life of God's people?**

We forget quickly. Fear, hardship, and time all erode our grip on what God has done. Remembering rightly is how faith stays anchored to reality instead of being blown around by present circumstances. Israel's failures came from forgetting; their courage was meant to come from remembering. The same is true for us. This is why God repeatedly builds memorials, festivals, and retellings into the life of His people.

*If you want to draw it out:*
- What happens to a person, or a group, that stops remembering what God has done?
- How is biblical "remembering" different from just having a good memory?

# Application

*Let these be personal and unhurried. Don't rush to fill silence; people often need a moment before they're willing to speak.*

**8. Looking back over the last year or two, what are some ways you have seen God's faithfulness that should strengthen your trust in Him today?**

The aim is for people to name specific evidence of God's care, the way Moses named the Red Sea and the wilderness, so that memory becomes fuel for present trust. Be ready to share your own example first to set the tone.

---

**★ 9. Is there an area of obedience, responsibility, or trust where you are currently tempted to focus more on the obstacles than on God's character? How does this passage challenge your thinking?**

The Kadesh generation measured the giants instead of measuring God. Help the group name the "giants" they're staring at right now, and then turn the question back to what they know to be true about God, just as Moses did in 1:29-33. The goal is not guilt but a redirected gaze.

# Leader's Note

The two halves of this study hold together: God's unwavering faithfulness and Israel's persistent unbelief. Keep returning to that contrast. Every reminder Moses gives is aimed at one outcome, that this new generation would trust and obey where their parents would not. Help your group feel that they, too, are standing at a threshold.
