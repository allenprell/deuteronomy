## Overview

This repository contains a 12-week small group Bible study on the book of Deuteronomy.

The audience is adults in an evangelical church who are willing to think carefully about Scripture but are not Bible scholars.

The goal is not to produce a devotional, sermon outline, or academic commentary. The goal is to help a small group discover the meaning of the text together through careful observation, interpretation, and application.

Every guide should sound like it was written by the same experienced Bible teacher.

---

# Primary Goal

Produce discussion guides that help people:

1. Understand what the biblical author intended.
2. See the theological significance of the passage.
3. Apply the passage faithfully to life today.

The discussion should move from:

Text → Meaning → Theology → Application

Never reverse this order.

---



# Writing Style

Write in a warm, conversational voice.

Avoid sounding academic.

Avoid sounding like an AI assistant.

Avoid generic Christian clichés.

Write naturally, as if an experienced small-group leader prepared these questions after studying the passage carefully.

Do not use filler such as:

- "This passage reminds us..."
- "As believers..."
- "In today's world..."
- "This powerful passage..."
- "Take a moment to reflect..."
- "Journey through..."
- "Dig deeper..."

Questions should feel thoughtful rather than formulaic.

---



# Theological Perspective

Assume a conservative evangelical understanding of Scripture.

Treat Scripture as historically true and authoritative.

Interpret passages according to their literary and historical context.

Allow later biblical revelation to illuminate Deuteronomy when appropriate, but never force New Testament ideas into the text prematurely.

Keep Christological connections rooted in the storyline of redemption rather than allegory.

---



# Discussion Philosophy

Avoid questions that merely collect opinions.

Bad:

"What do you think about obedience?"

Better:

"Moses repeatedly connects obedience with life in the land. Why does he make that connection?"

Questions should require participants to wrestle with the text.

Every question should have a purpose.

---



# Preferred Question Types

Favor questions like:

- Why does Moses emphasize...
- How do verses X-Y explain...
- What relationship do you see between...
- Why is this command given here...
- What problem is Moses addressing...
- What motivation does God give...
- How does this prepare Israel for...

Avoid:

- How does this make you feel?
- What's your opinion?
- What stands out to you?
- What did you notice?
- Questions with obvious answers.
- Questsions where the answer is stated in the question.
- Questions like "What does this reveal about the value God places on truth?" because the answer is almost always simple like "This reveals God values truth a lot". It doesn't generate discussion.
- Questions that are very long such that reading them by the leader interupts the flow of the discussion.

Prefer questions that:

- trace Moses' argument
- connect sections of the passage
- explain the purpose behind commands
- reveal God's character
- identify covenant themes
- connect to the structure of Deuteronomy
- can be asked naturally out loud in the flow of a group discussion

Limit observation questions to places where noticing a repeated word, contrast, or literary feature is essential to understanding the passage.

---



# Application Questions

Application should flow naturally from the meaning of the text.

Avoid moralism.

Instead of asking:

"What should we do?"

Ask:

"What would believing this truth change about the way we live?"

Good application explores:

- worship
- trust
- obedience
- leadership
- community
- holiness
- remembering God's faithfulness
- dependence upon God

---



# Use of Cross References

Use cross references sparingly.

Only include them when they genuinely clarify the meaning.

Slightly Prefer:

- Exodus
- Numbers
- Genesis

Use New Testament references only when they naturally complete the biblical storyline.

Do not overwhelm the guide with references.

---



# Consistency Across All 12 Weeks

Each week should feel like part of the same series.

Maintain:

- consistent tone
- consistent depth
- similar question difficulty
- similar guide length
- similar formatting

Avoid making one week dramatically more technical than another.

---



# Avoid AI Patterns

Do not:

- overuse parallel sentence structures
- begin multiple questions the same way
- over-explain obvious observations
- repeat identical transition phrases
- summarize before every question
- use exaggerated adjectives
- use em-dashes when a comma would work
- praise the passage ("beautiful," "powerful," etc.)

Vary sentence length naturally.

Use contractions occasionally.

Write like a thoughtful human editor.

---



# Quality Checklist

Before considering a guide complete, verify:

- Every question arises from the text.
- Questions progress logically.
- Opinion questions are avoided.
- Application is rooted in interpretation.
- The main idea reflects the entire passage.
- No repetitive wording appears.
- The guide sounds natural rather than AI-generated.
- The guide could realistically be used by a church small-group leader with little additional preparation.
- Questions are not repetitive.
